Teaching Children the 99 Names of Allah

✍️ By NurulBayt Editorial Team 📅 June 2026 ⏱ 6 min read

The 99 Names of Allah (Asma-ul-Husna) are among the most beautiful gifts Islam has given us. Each name reveals a different aspect of Allah's perfection — His mercy, His power, His love, His wisdom. Teaching these names to children is not just about memorization; it's about helping them know Allah personally, intimately, and lovingly. When children understand who Allah is through His names, their faith deepens, their hearts soften, and their connection with their Creator becomes unshakeable. Here are fun, simple, and effective ways to teach your children the 99 Names of Allah.

1. Start with the Most Relatable Names

Not all 99 names are equally easy for young children to grasp. Start with the names that speak directly to their hearts — names of love, mercy, and care.

Perfect Starting Names:

  • Ar-Rahman (The Most Merciful): "Allah is so merciful, He gives us everything — the sun, the rain, our families."
  • Ar-Raheem (The Especially Merciful): "Allah's mercy is special — it's always there for us."
  • Al-Wadud (The Most Loving): "Allah loves you more than anyone in the whole world."
  • Al-Ghaffar (The Forgiving): "When we make mistakes and say sorry, Allah forgives us."
  • Al-Khaliq (The Creator): "Allah made everything — the stars, the trees, the animals, and you!"
  • Ar-Razzaq (The Provider): "Allah gives us our food, our home, and everything we need."

Once children are comfortable with these, gradually introduce more names as they grow older and their understanding deepens.

💡 Practical Tip:

Don't rush. It's better for a child to deeply understand 10 names than to superficially memorize all 99. Quality over quantity, always.

2. Use Stories to Bring Names to Life

Children are natural story-lovers. Use this to your advantage by connecting each name to a story from the Quran or the life of the Prophet ﷺ.

Examples:

  • Al-Hafiz (The Protector): Tell the story of Prophet Ibrahim (AS) in the fire — how Allah protected him.
  • Al-Fattah (The Opener): Share the story of how Allah opened the sea for Prophet Musa (AS).
  • As-Salam (The Source of Peace): Talk about how the Prophet ﷺ brought peace to Makkah.
  • Al-Latif (The Subtle One): Share how Allah subtly provided for Maryam (AS) with fruits out of season.

Stories make abstract concepts concrete. When children hear how Allah's names manifested in real events, they develop a living, breathing understanding of who Allah is.

3. Create a "Name of the Week" Routine

Consistency is the secret to learning. Dedicate one name per week and explore it deeply as a family.

Weekly Routine Ideas:

  • Monday: Introduce the name — its Arabic spelling, pronunciation, and meaning
  • Tuesday: Share a story related to this name
  • Wednesday: Find examples of this name in nature and daily life
  • Thursday: Make dua using this name together as a family
  • Friday: Create a craft or artwork featuring this name
  • Weekend: Review and celebrate what you've learned

At this pace, you'll cover all 99 names in about 2 years — a beautiful, lifelong journey with your children.

4. Turn Learning into Play

Play is the language of children. When learning feels like play, children absorb information effortlessly.

Fun Activities to Try:

  • Name Matching Game: Create cards with Arabic names on one set and English meanings on another. Have children match them.
  • Allah's Names Bingo: Create bingo cards with different names. Call out meanings, and children mark the corresponding name.
  • Scavenger Hunt: "Find something that shows Allah is Al-Khaliq (The Creator)!" Children search the house or garden.
  • Name Charades: Act out the meaning of a name and have others guess which name it is.
  • Song and Rhyme: Create simple songs or rhymes for groups of names. Music aids memorization beautifully.

These activities make learning joyful and memorable. Children will ask for "more name games" instead of resisting learning.

💡 Practical Tip:

Keep sessions short — 10-15 minutes for young children, 20-30 minutes for older ones. End while they're still having fun, and they'll always want more.

5. Connect Names to Daily Life

The most powerful way to teach Allah's names is to weave them into daily conversations. When children hear these names in natural contexts, they internalize them.

Daily Connection Examples:

  • At meals: "Alhamdulillah! Allah is Ar-Razzaq — He provided this delicious food for us."
  • When seeing nature: "SubhanAllah! Look at this beautiful flower. Allah is Al-Khaliq — He created it."
  • When someone is sick: "Let's make dua. Allah is Ash-Shafi — The Healer."
  • When feeling scared: "Allah is Al-Hafiz — The Protector. He's always watching over us."
  • When making a mistake: "Allah is Al-Ghaffar — The Forgiving. Let's ask Him to forgive us."
  • When receiving a gift: "Allah is Al-Wahhab — The Giver. He loves to give us good things."

When children see Allah's names reflected in their everyday experiences, their understanding becomes deep and personal.

6. Use Visual Aids and Crafts

Visual learning is powerful, especially for young children. Create beautiful visual reminders of Allah's names throughout your home.

Craft Ideas:

  • Name Posters: Create colorful posters with each name in Arabic, transliteration, and meaning. Hang them around the house.
  • Name Booklet: Help your child create their own "99 Names Booklet" with illustrations for each name.
  • Name Stones: Paint small stones with Arabic names. Children can hold them during dua time.
  • Name Garland: Create a decorative garland with names for Ramadan or Islamic occasions.
  • Name Puzzle: Create puzzles where each piece has a name, and the completed puzzle reveals a beautiful Islamic design.

These visual aids serve as constant reminders and make learning tangible and engaging.

7. Recite and Repeat with Love

Repetition is the mother of learning. The Prophet ﷺ loved to repeat important phrases three times. Follow his example with Allah's names.

Effective Repetition Methods:

  • After Salah: Recite a few names together as a family
  • During car rides: Play nasheeds or recitations of the 99 names
  • Before sleeping: Recite names as part of the bedtime routine
  • During meals: Take turns saying one name each

Make the repetition rhythmic and beautiful. Children naturally love patterns and rhythms — use this to your advantage.

8. Make Dua Using the Names

The ultimate purpose of learning Allah's names is to call upon Him through them. Teach your children to use the names in their duas.

Examples of Dua with Names:

  • "Ya Rahman, have mercy on us."
  • "Ya Ghaffar, forgive our mistakes."
  • "Ya Razzaq, provide for our family."
  • "Ya Hafiz, protect us from harm."
  • "Ya Hadi, guide us to the right path."
  • "Ya Shafi, heal our sick loved ones."

When children experience the power of calling upon Allah by His names, their faith becomes alive and personal. They learn that Allah is not distant — He is close, responsive, and loving.

Final Thoughts

Teaching children the 99 Names of Allah is one of the most beautiful investments you can make in their spiritual development. It's not a race to memorize all 99 — it's a lifelong journey of knowing Allah more deeply. Start small, be consistent, and most importantly, let your own love for Allah's names shine through. When children see a parent who is in awe of Allah's beauty, they will naturally want to know Him too. May Allah make our children among those who know Him, love Him, and call upon Him by His beautiful names. Ameen.

Frequently Asked Questions

At what age should I start teaching my child the 99 names? +
You can start introducing simple names like Ar-Rahman and Al-Wadud from age 3-4. More structured learning can begin around age 5-6. The key is to make it age-appropriate, fun, and meaningful at every stage.
How long does it take to teach all 99 names? +
There's no rush! At a pace of one name per week, it takes about 2 years. But the goal isn't speed — it's deep understanding. Some families take 3-5 years, and that's perfectly fine. Quality matters more than quantity.
What if my child loses interest? +
Switch up your approach! Try a new game, craft, or story. Take a break if needed and return later. Most importantly, examine your own enthusiasm — children mirror our energy. Show your own love for Allah's names, and they'll naturally follow.
